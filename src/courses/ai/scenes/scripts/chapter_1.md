- On the right, the text morphs:
  - First: `Scanning attributes...`
  - Then: `Height: 180 cm`, `Weight: 75 kg`
  - Finally: It morphs into a glowing, bold vector representation: **`[180, 75]`**

### 🎙️ Narration (Voiceover)
**[0:30 - 1:15]**
"How does a computer understand a person? Computers don't see skin, bones, or personality. They see data. 

Let's start simple. How do we turn a person into numbers? 

We take measurements. Let's measure their height: one hundred and eighty centimeters. And their weight: seventy-five kilograms. 

By taking these two simple features, we've captured two essential characteristics of this person. But we don't keep them as separate labels. We group them together into a list of ordered numbers: **`[180, 75]`**.

This isn't just a list. It is a set of instructions. It's an address."

## 🎬 Scene 3: People Become Locations

- The vertical Y-axis is labeled **"Weight (kg)"** (ranging from 30 to 110).

  - **Alex** at `[180, 75]` (Tall, average weight)
  - **Bianca** at `[155, 48]` (Shorter, lighter)
  - **Charlie** at `[172, 95]` (Tall, heavier)
  - **Dana** at `[162, 62]` (Medium height and weight)
- The sidebar shows: *"People Become Locations. Plotted on axes, people become coordinates in space. Similar people naturally cluster close to each other."*

### 🎙️ Narration (Voiceover)
**[1:15 - 2:05]**
"Once we have this pair of numbers—one hundred and eighty, and seventy-five—we can plot them. 

Let's make Height our horizontal axis, and Weight our vertical axis. 

If we plot our first person, Alex, at `[180, 75]`, Alex is no longer just a person in our database—Alex is a *location* in this 2D space. 

If we do the same for Bianca, Charlie, and Dana, look at what happens. Each person becomes a coordinate. But notice something even more interesting: people who are similar in build naturally end up closer to each other in this space. 

By translating features into coordinates, we've created a map where physical similarity equals spatial closeness."

## 🎬 Scene 7: Images Are Numbers
### 🎥 Visuals on Screen
- A grid displays three versions of the same cat image side-by-side:
  1. **Original Image**: A high-quality photo of a cat.
  2. **Pixel Grid**: The image zoomed into a 16x16 pixelated layout.
  3. **Stored Values**: The same 16x16 grid, but every pixel shows a raw brightness number (e.g., 234, 181, 92).
- The teacher hovers over different pixels in the grid, showing a tooltip detailing the RGB color coordinates and the average brightness stored value.
- Below, a banner highlights: **`16 × 16 × 3 = 768 values`**

### 🎙️ Narration (Voiceover)
**[5:10 - 6:05]**
"Let's look at images. How does a computer see a cat? 

To us, it’s a cute furry face. But if we zoom in, the image is just a grid of pixels. For a small 16-by-16 grid, we have 256 pixels. Since color is made of Red, Green, and Blue, each pixel needs three numbers. 

16 times 16 times 3 gives us 768 numbers. 

So, this tiny image of a cat is actually a **768-dimensional point**! 

When an AI compares two images, it isn't looking at 'eyes' or 'whiskers' initially; it is comparing two points in a 768-dimensional space."

### 🎙️ Narration (Voiceover)
**[7:00 - 7:50]**
"Now, we can finally call all of these lists of numbers by their real name. 

In mathematics and computer science, an ordered list of numbers is called a **vector**. 

Whether it is a 2D coordinate like height and weight, a 768-dimensional representation of a cat, or a 1,536-dimensional representation of a sentence's meaning—these are all vectors. 

A vector is the foundational building block of modern Artificial Intelligence. It is the bridge between human concepts and computer math. Everything—movies, sounds, words, images, and people—can be represented as a vector."
