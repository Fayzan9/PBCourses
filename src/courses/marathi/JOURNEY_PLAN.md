# Marathi Fundamentals — Course Journey Plan

## Overview
A 12-chapter interactive course teaching Marathi from zero to advanced fluency.
Each chapter follows the same pedagogical arc:
**Hook → Concept Build-Up → Visualization/Interactivity → Practice → Next Hook.**

Marathi-specific pedagogy notes:
- **Script first** — learners master Devanagari before vocabulary, since Marathi sounds don't map well to Roman transliteration.
- **Gender is woven in early** — Marathi has three grammatical genders (masculine, feminine, neuter); every noun and verb agreement lesson references this from Chapter 2 onward.
- **SOV word order** is introduced in Chapter 2 and reinforced every chapter so it becomes instinctive.
- **Postpositions not prepositions** — flagged explicitly when English learners would reach for "in / on / to".
- **Honorific tiers** — informal `तू`, polite `तुम्ही`, and formal `आपण` introduced in Chapter 2 and practiced throughout.

## Pedagogical Design Notes

### Script display convention
Every scene with Marathi text should show three tiers:
1. **Devanagari** — the primary script (largest)
2. **Romanisation** — IAST-based for pronunciation (smaller, muted)
3. **English gloss** — word-for-word undergloss, then natural translation

### Complexity of the ergative construction (Chapter 5)
The transitive past tense is the single hardest concept for English speakers. Dedicate extra visualizer time to the split: *intransitive past = subject agreement*, *transitive past = object agreement*. Use a split-screen animated diagram with a clear visual "trigger rule."

### Gender agreement threading
Gender is introduced in Chapter 3 and must be reinforced in every subsequent chapter. Every conjugation table, adjective table, and possessive table should include a gender column — never collapse it for brevity.

### Honorific register threading
Three-tier formality (तू / तुम्ही / आपण) introduced in Chapter 2. Each conversation sandbox should specify the register required — learners should not default to informal.

### Audio-first design
Devanagari is phonetic but Marathi has retroflex consonants and short/long vowel distinctions that text alone cannot convey. Every word introduced in Chapters 1–3 must have a tap-to-hear audio affordance. From Chapter 4 onward, assume the learner can decode the script.

### Cultural grounding
Marathi language is inseparable from Maharashtrian culture. Each chapter's Hook should feature a real cultural touchstone (a market, a festival, a proverb, a writer). Avoid clinical grammar-textbook openers.
